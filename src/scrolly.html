When Columbia students arrive on campus, they enter a neighborhood, like all New York City neighborhoods, with its own civic and political landscape. In West Harlem, that landscape has been the focal point of debates over the University’s expanding landownership, restricted campus access, and impact on surrounding neighborhoods.

The Neighborhood

Columbia sits in West Harlem, the neighborhood that makes up Manhattan’s Community District 9, which stretches from 110th Street to 155th Street and runs from the Hudson River on the west to Manhattan Avenue and Edgecombe Avenue on the east.

District 9 is represented by Community Board 9, one of 59 advisory boards across the city. While the boards do not pass laws, they make recommendations on land use, zoning, liquor licenses, and city budget priorities. CB9 board members are appointed by Manhattan Borough President Mark Levine, with at least half nominated by New York City Council members. Members serve as unpaid city officials, and the board is currently chaired by Victor Edwards.

CB9 is divided into 10 standing committees that meet monthly, in addition to ad-hoc committees. These include arts and culture, the cannabis task force, economic development, health and environment, housing, land use and zoning, landmarks preservation and parks, senior action, the SGL/LGBTQ task force, strategic planning, uniformed services and transportation, and youth, education, and libraries. New York City residents can attend committee meetings, testify during hearings, and provide input on city services and local development projects.

The neighborhood is represented in the City Council by District 7 City Council member Shaun Abreu, CC ’14. District 7 spans the north of Manhattan, from the Upper West Side through Morningside Heights, West Harlem, Manhattan Valley, Manhattanville, Hamilton Heights, and Washington Heights. Abreu won the Democratic nomination for his seat during the citywide primary election in June. He won two previous City Council races in 2021 and 2023.

Community District 9 covers three neighborhoods: Morningside Heights, Manhattanville, and Hamilton Heights.

Morningside Heights is home to Columbia’s Morningside campus, Barnard College, Teachers College, the Manhattan School of Music, the Union Theological Seminary, and the Jewish Theological Seminary. It also contains Riverside Church, the Cathedral of St. John the Divine, the Grant Houses, Morningside Gardens, and two major parks. Due to its concentration of globally renowned institutions and architecture, Morningside Heights has been dubbed the “acropolis of the new world.”

The Expansion

Just north of Morningside Heights is Manhattanville, which has become central to the University’s expansion. Once a Dutch settlement and later a Revolutionary War site, it is now the location of Columbia’s new Manhattanville campus.

The University first announced its plans to redevelop 17 acres of the neighborhood into a new campus in 2003, under then-University President Lee Bollinger. According to Columbia’s master plan for the development, the Manhattanville expansion is meant to provide “innovative academic space” to keep the University at the forefront of global research, foster cross-disciplinary collaboration, and create an open, sustainable campus that strengthens ties with the surrounding community.

Construction began in 2008 after New York state approved Columbia’s eminent domain pursuits—despite objections from activists, residents, and business owners concerned about displacement, gentrification, and the loss of neighborhood character. The first buildings opened up in 2017, including facilities for the arts and sciences.

Manhattanville is also home to the City College of New York. For many residents, Columbia’s growth here symbolizes the broader tension between economic development and the risk of displacement. Recently state legislators reintroduced a bill to revoke the University’s authorization for the Manhattanville expansion amid longstanding concerns that its commitment to affordable housing and community benefits have fallen short.

A 2024 analysis by the Community Service Society found that in the ten years following the state’s eminent domain approvals, the neighborhood’s Black population decreased by 14 percent, its Hispanic population decreased by 10 percent, and the area saw a loss of nearly 6,000 rent-regulated units between 2010 and 2020.

At the northern end of the district is Hamilton Heights, which includes the historic, culturally significant Sugar Hill neighborhood. Hamilton Heights also houses City College’s north campus, Hamilton Grange, Riverbank State Park, and the Dance Theatre of Harlem.

Hamilton Heights has been the site of recent disputes over supportive housing—housing communities built by the city to support individuals transitioning out of drug abuse, homelessness, or other difficult situations. Some community members argue that the neighborhood has taken on more than its share of such facilities, in comparison to other parts of the city. The conflict arose after the city began development on another supportive housing building in the area without CB9 approval.

Columbia’s Commitments to the Community

These shifting neighborhood dynamics in Upper Manhattan are tied to Columbia’s position as the city’s largest private landowner. Because the University is an educational nonprofit, it is largely exempt from property taxes, a benefit valued at about $170 million per year.

To address community concerns over its continued land expansion but lack of community investment due to tax exemption, Columbia signed the Community Benefits Agreement in 2009, promising $170 million in investments over 36 years. That commitment, coordinated and distributed through an independent nonprofit, the West Harlem Development Corporation, amounts to roughly $4.7 million annually.

The pledged community benefits includes 5,000 square feet of arts space for use by local residents, $76 million in a benefits fund, $20 million for affordable housing, and $20 million in in-kind benefits, or access to campus service. Sixteen years later, some community leaders and artists say that many of these commitments have yet to be fully realized. Spectator has previously spoken to artists and community leaders who described limited access to affordable studio and exhibition space, noting that only $400,000 of the $20 million allocated for campus use had been spent amid delays and restrictions.

A 2023 Spectator analysis found that Columbia’s annual tax exemptions outpace its annual Community Benefits Agreement and local infrastructure investments by over $150 million each year.

Over the past two years, New York City politicians and students have been advocating for the passage of a bill in the New York Legislature that seeks to secure additional funding for the City University of New York system by revoking the tax-exempt statuses of Columbia and New York University.

Columbia, however, has emphasized that the CBA represents a long-term commitment to the community. A University spokesperson previously told Spectator that the CBA focuses on “meaningful investments that provide local jobs and economic opportunity, along with sustainable community partnerships built around the University’s intellectual capital and the work of thousands of committed faculty and students.”

Residents in the area have previously criticized Columbia’s role in decreasing affordability and changing the neighborhood’s demographics: Due to the University’s status as a nonprofit landowning institution, it has the ability to convert rent-stabilized units into affiliate housing once the primary tenant dies, rather than putting the unit back onto the market in its rent-stabilized status. As a result, the local community consistently faces displacement and rising prices.

As the landowner of the Red Balloon Learning Center, a University-affiliated daycare school serving West Harlem and Morningside Heights, Columbia informed the school community in 2022 that it would not be renewing the center’s lease. Out of the six Columbia-affiliated centers that offered over nine hours for five days a week for daycare, Red Balloon charged the second-lowest monthly tuition rate for toddlers. Parents and staff described the decision as devastating, worrying that it would eliminate a rare community-centered child care option for both Columbia affiliates and local families.

At the time, Columbia stated that the decision was based on “expectations for Columbia-affiliated early learning centers and came after years of working with the center.” The University added that it would provide information and resources to all families seeking a new program, regardless of their affiliation with Columbia, but did not specify a reason for ending Red Balloon’s lease.

In March 2025, Columbia announced plans to open a new childcare center at 560 Riverside Drive.

The Gates

As of late, campus access has become another point of conflict in the neighborhood.

In October 2023, the University closed its gates to anyone without a Columbia ID, thereby cutting off community members from College Walk, the essential pedestrian path that connects Broadway to Amsterdam Avenue. The University initially restricted campus access ahead of planned demonstrations over the war in Gaza. Campus access has remained limited ever since, sparking a debate—and a lawsuit—over private versus public space and Columbia’s role in the neighborhood.

While Columbia officials said the closures were for safety reasons, residents and elected leaders objected, citing the impact on seniors and people with disabilities. In January a group of local residents filed a class action lawsuit against the University, claiming the closures violated both the Americans with Disabilities Act and a decades-old easement agreement. That agreement, written in 1953 when the city gave Columbia control of the section of 116th Street that is now College Walk, required that the block remain open to the public as a pedestrian walkway.

CB9 has taken an active role in responding to these conflicts. In February it voted against renewing Columbia’s liquor license for Lerner Hall, framing the decision as a response to the continued gate closures. CB9 Chair Victor Edwards, alongside City Council member Shaun Abreu, called on Columbia to reopen its gates in an op-ed published in the New York Daily News on Jan. 15.

“This closure is more than an inconvenience; it is a breach of trust and a violation of the agreements that govern this historic pathway,” they wrote.

The board has also condemned the arrest of Mahmoud Khalil, SIPA ’24, by Immigration and Customs Enforcement, arguing that the case raised concerns about free speech and due process.

CB9 will resume committee meetings in September, after a summer recess, allowing students and residents alike to provide input in local community issues.
